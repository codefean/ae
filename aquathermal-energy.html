What is Aquathermal Energy?

Aquathermal energy uses the relatively stable temperature of rivers, canals, lakes, ponds, and coastal waters as a source or sink for heat. Combined with a heat pump, it can provide low-carbon heating and cooling for individual buildings or shared energy networks.

What it is

A water-source heat exchange system

Aquathermal systems exchange thermal energy with a nearby water body and use an electrically driven heat pump to bring that energy to a useful temperature. The water does not need to be hot: even cool water contains low-temperature energy that a heat pump can upgrade for space heating and hot water.

In cooling mode, the process is reversed. Excess heat is removed from a building and transferred toward the water-side circuit. The source water and building circuit normally remain separate, and the system can serve one property, a group of buildings, or a district energy network.

Local source

Uses thermal energy available in a nearby river, canal, lake, pond, or coastal water body.

Heat pump

Raises low-temperature energy to the level needed by the building or heat network.

Heating or cooling

Moves energy into a building in winter and can move unwanted heat out in warmer periods.

How it works

Four steps between water and building

Connect

An intake or submerged loop connects the system thermally to the water body.

Exchange

A heat exchanger transfers energy while keeping the source-water and building circuits separate.

Upgrade

An electric heat pump raises the captured energy to a useful heating temperature.

Distribute

Heat is delivered to rooms, hot-water systems, or a shared low-temperature network.

Heating mode: thermal energy moves from the water body into the building or network.

Collect

The building circuit absorbs excess heat from rooms, equipment, or processes.

Reverse

The heat-pump cycle reverses, or direct cooling is used where conditions allow.

Transfer

A heat exchanger moves the collected energy toward the water-side circuit.

Release

Heat is discharged within ecological, monitoring, and permit requirements.

Cooling mode: thermal energy moves out of the building and toward the water source.

System configurations

Open-loop and closed-loop systems

Both configurations exchange heat with water, but they connect to the source differently. The right design depends on local hydrology, ecology, energy demand, available space, regulation, and lifecycle cost.

Configuration 01

Open loop

Source water is abstracted, passed through a heat exchanger, and returned to the water body at a controlled temperature difference.

  • Provides direct thermal contact with the source
  • Requires filtration and carefully designed intake infrastructure
  • Must address abstraction, discharge, ecology, and permitting
Configuration 02

Closed loop

A sealed pipe or coil is submerged in the water, allowing heat transfer without drawing source water into the system.

  • Avoids direct water abstraction and discharge
  • Usually reduces filtration requirements
  • Performance depends on loop area, placement, and water movement

Neither configuration is universally preferable. System design must respond to the water body, the building or network, environmental constraints, and the organisations responsible for long-term operation.
