Research focus

The social conditions of place-based energy transitions

Aquathermal energy can provide stable, low-carbon heating and cooling by harnessing thermal energy from rivers, canals, ponds, and coastal waters. This research examines the social and governance conditions that shape whether such projects gain legitimacy and progress to implementation. Situated within the EU’s broader energy security and affordability crisis, the study highlights the growing need for H/C systems that are technically viable, affordable, equitable, and publicly supported.

Research questions

How do social limitations to adaptation manifest in aquathermal energy development projects?

How can governance arrangements better integrate social, institutional, and cultural dimensions of change?

Analytical framework

Four social limitations to adaptation

The framework developed by Adger et al. (2008) identifies barriers that cannot be addressed through engineering, economics, or information alone. Here, it is applied in the context of the EU energy crisis to examine whether social resilience can be measured—a long-standing debate in sustainability science.

01 · How and what we value

Ethics

Who benefits, who decides, who bears cost and risk, and whether fairness and vulnerable groups are meaningfully considered.

02 · How and what we know

Knowledge

Whether technical, regulatory, and local knowledge is available, and whether uncertainty is adaptively managed.

03 · How and what we perceive

Risk perception

How climate, energy, cost, autonomy, and infrastructure risks are understood by institutions and communities.

04 · How and why we live

Culture & values

How projects align with place identity, local norms, water relationships, heritage, collective action, and energy autonomy.

Findings

Social resilience is an implementation condition

Social limits shaped every case.

Ethics, knowledge, risk perception, and cultural values influenced legitimacy, continuity, and implementation.

Institutions navigated bureaucracy better.

University, municipal, and port-authority backing helped mobilize expertise and interpret permitting requirements.

Community-led projects built stronger legitimacy.

Citizen-led initiatives more consistently foregrounded fairness, inclusion, local identity, autonomy, and lived experience.

Neither model was sufficient alone.

Institutional competence did not automatically create trust, while community legitimacy could not overcome regulatory or financial uncertainty alone.

Governance recommendations

Bridge institutional influence & community legitimacy

Recommendation 01

Build hybrid governance from the outset

Combine institutional resources, regulatory competence, and continuity with genuine community influence, local knowledge, and accountability. Participation should be built into programme design and funding criteria.

Recommendation 02

Evaluate social limitations throughout the project lifecycle

Revisit ethical goals, knowledge gaps, risk narratives, and cultural alignment as a project moves from early framing to long-term operation.

Scope and limitations

The evidence comes from six qualitative cases in one interregional North Sea programme, so the findings identify transferable patterns rather than universal rules. National regulation, political culture, institutional capacity, and social trust will affect how the recommendations work in practice. The study also highlights tension between meaningful participation and the speed demanded by the climate and energy transition.

Aquathermal Energy Explained

Aquathermal energy uses the relatively stable temperature of rivers, canals, lakes, ponds, and coastal waters as a source or sink for heat. Combined with a heat pump, it can provide low-carbon heating and cooling for individual buildings or shared energy networks.

What it is

A water-source heat exchange system

Aquathermal systems exchange thermal energy with a nearby water body and use an electrically driven heat pump to bring that energy to a useful temperature. The water does not need to be hot: even cool water contains low-temperature energy that a heat pump can upgrade for space heating and hot water.

In cooling mode, the process is reversed. Excess heat is removed from a building and transferred toward the water-side circuit. The source water and building circuit normally remain separate, and the system can serve one property, a group of buildings, or a district energy network.

Local source

Uses thermal energy available in a nearby river, canal, lake, pond, or coastal water body.

Heat pump

Raises low-temperature energy to the level needed by the building or heat network.

Heating or cooling

Moves energy into a building in winter and can move unwanted heat out in warmer periods.

How it works

Four steps between water and building

Connect

An intake or submerged loop connects the system thermally to the water body.

Exchange

A heat exchanger transfers energy while keeping the source-water and building circuits separate.

Upgrade

An electric heat pump raises the captured energy to a useful heating temperature.

Distribute

Heat is delivered to rooms, hot-water systems, or a shared low-temperature network.

Heating mode: thermal energy moves from the water body into the building or network.

Collect

The building circuit absorbs excess heat from rooms, equipment, or processes.

Reverse

The heat-pump cycle reverses, or direct cooling is used where conditions allow.

Transfer

A heat exchanger moves the collected energy toward the water-side circuit.

Release

Heat is discharged within ecological, monitoring, and permit requirements.

Cooling mode: thermal energy moves out of the building and toward the water source.

System configurations

Open-loop and closed-loop systems

Both configurations exchange heat with water, but they connect to the source differently. The right design depends on local hydrology, ecology, energy demand, available space, regulation, and lifecycle cost.

Configuration 01

Open loop

Source water is abstracted, passed through a heat exchanger, and returned to the water body at a controlled temperature difference.

  • Provides direct thermal contact with the source
  • Requires filtration and carefully designed intake infrastructure
  • Must address abstraction, discharge, ecology, and permitting
Configuration 02

Closed loop

A sealed pipe or coil is submerged in the water, allowing heat transfer without drawing source water into the system.

  • Avoids direct water abstraction and discharge
  • Usually reduces filtration requirements
  • Performance depends on loop area, placement, and water movement

Neither configuration is universally preferable. System design must respond to the water body, the building or network, environmental constraints, and the organisations responsible for long-term operation.
